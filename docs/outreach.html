How to Send a Letter to Every Address in Our Service Area

The most cost-effective way to blanket your service area with mail is the USPS Every Door Direct Mail (EDDM) program. It lets you send postcards, flyers, or letters to every household on specific postal routes—no mailing list or individual addresses needed.

Step-by-Step: EDDM for 4 Feathers

1

Create a USPS.com Account

Sign up free at usps.com/business/every-door-direct-mail. No special mailing permit is needed for EDDM Retail.

2

Map Your Routes

Use the EDDM Online Tool to select ZIP codes in your service area: Blanchard, Norman, Moore, Mustang, Yukon, Edmond, Chickasha, Purcell, OKC. Filter by household income, age, and size to target homeowners most likely to need fencing.

3

Design Your Mailpiece

Create an oversized postcard (6.25"×9" or larger—oversized gets 5% response vs 3.5% for letters). Include:

  • Headline offer: "FREE Fence Estimate — Spring Special: $200 Off Any Install Over $2,000"
  • Before/after project photo
  • QR code linking to your contact page or quote form
  • Phone number: (405) 276-9765
  • Service list: Wood, Chain Link, Vinyl, Wrought Iron, Deck Building, Repairs
  • "Locally Owned • Fully Insured • 10+ Years Experience"
4

Print Your Postcards

Use a local print shop or online printer (VistaPrint, GotPrint, PostcardMania). Bulk pricing: roughly $0.04–$0.15 per postcard depending on quantity and finish.

5

Pay Postage & Bundle

EDDM Retail postage is $0.247 per piece (as of 2026). Minimum 200 pieces, max 5,000 per ZIP per day. Bundle in groups of 50–100, attach the EDDM facing slip from your USPS receipt.

6

Drop Off at Post Office

Take your bundled, labeled mail to the Post Office(s) that serve your selected routes. USPS delivers to every door on those routes within a few business days.

Key Takeaways

  • Direct mail gets opened 90% of the time vs 20–30% for email. People keep postcards for an average of 17 days.
  • Response rate for direct mail (4.9%–9%) is up to 9x higher than email (~1%).
  • 75% of people recall a brand after receiving direct mail, vs only 44% after a digital ad.
  • For 5,000 postcards at ~$1,800 total: expect 50–150 responses, and 25–75 qualified leads. If you close 30% of leads on a $3,000 average job, that's 8–23 jobs = $24,000–$69,000 in revenue from an $1,800 investment.

Recommended Outreach Strategy for 4 Feathers

Cost: $25–$100 per successful referral  |  Impact: Highest quality leads

  • Offer past customers $50 (or a gift card) for every referral that books a job

Cost: Free (time)  |  Impact: Medium-High

  • Post seasonal tips: "Storm damage? We do emergency fence repairs" or "Spring is the best time to install a new fence"

Tier 2: Direct Mail Campaign (Medium Cost, High Impact)

5. EDDM Postcard Campaign

Cost: ~$1,800 for 5,000 postcards  |  Impact: High — expected 50–150 responses

  • Follow the step-by-step EDDM process above
  • Timing matters: Send in early spring (March–April) and early fall (September–October)—peak fence season

6. Door Hangers Around Active Job Sites

Cost: ~$0.10–$0.25 per hanger  |  Impact: High in targeted areas

  • Every time you're on a job, hang 50–100 door hangers on surrounding homes
  • "Your neighbor just got a new fence from us! Ask us for a free quote"
  • Neighbors see your crew working—they're already primed, this closes the deal
  • Much cheaper than EDDM when used in targeted micro-areas

Tier 3: Paid Digital Advertising (Scale When Ready)

Cost: Time only  |  Impact: Compounds over time

Cost: Merchant fee (2–5%)  |  Impact: Increases close rate by 20–30%

How to Track Results

To know which outreach methods work best, track every lead:

  • Ask every caller: "How did you hear about us?" and write it down
  • Use a unique phone number on the postcard (Google Voice is free) to track direct mail calls separately
  • Use a unique URL or QR code on the postcard that goes to a dedicated landing page (e.g., 4feathersfencingandcontracting.com/postcard)
  • Track in a simple spreadsheet: Date, Name, Phone, Source (Postcard/Facebook/Google/Referral/Yard Sign), Quoted Amount, Sold (Y/N)
  • Calculate cost per lead and cost per closed job for each channel monthly
  • Double down on what works. Cut what doesn't.

Once You Have Leads: Call, Text, or Email?

Getting the lead is only half the battle. How fast and how you respond determines whether you win the job or lose it to the contractor who replied first.

The #1 Rule: Speed to Lead

The 5-Minute Rule (Harvard Business Review / MIT Research)

  • Responding within 5 minutes makes you 21x more likely to qualify the lead vs responding in 30 minutes
  • Responding within 5 minutes makes you 100x more likely to connect vs waiting 30 minutes
  • After 10 minutes, lead contact rates drop by 400%
  • 78% of customers buy from the company that responds first
  • The average contractor takes 42 hours to respond to a lead. Be the one who responds in 5 minutes and you win by default.

The Verdict: What Works Best?

Use ALL THREE—in This Order

  1. Call first (within 5 minutes). This is where jobs are won. The contractor who calls first wins 78% of the time.
  2. Text immediately if they don't answer. Texts have a 98% open rate and 45% response rate—far higher than any other method.
  3. Email for follow-up with your formal quote, portfolio photos, and company details. Email is documentation, not outreach.

Never rely on email alone for first contact. A 1–5% response rate on email means you'd need to send 100 emails to get 1–5 responses. One phone call in the first 5 minutes is worth more than 50 emails.

Bottom Line: Don't Cold Call Random People

Cold calling a list of random phone numbers has a 1–3% success rate, burns hours of time, and can violate Do Not Call registry laws (fines up to $43,792 per violation). Instead:

  • Let the platforms bring leads TO you (Thumbtack, Angi, Google LSA, Nextdoor)
  • Respond to those leads like your hair is on fire (5-min rule)
  • Use inbound marketing (EDDM postcards, yard signs, Facebook) to generate calls FROM interested people
  • Only "cold call" neighborhoods near your active job sites—that's warm enough to be effective
